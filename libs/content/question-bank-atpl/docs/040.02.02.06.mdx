# Integration of sensory inputs

## Learning Objectives

1.  **040.02.02.06.01** -- State the interaction between vision, equilibrium,
    proprioception and hearing to obtain spatial orientation in flight.
2.  **040.02.02.06.02** -- Define the term "illusion".
3.  **040.02.02.06.03** -- Give examples of visual illusions based on shape
    constancy, size constancy, aerial perspective, atmospheric perspective, the
    absence of focal or ambient cues, autokinesis, vectional false horizons,
    field myopia, and surface planes.
4.  **040.02.02.06.04** -- Relate these illusions to problems that may be
    experienced in flight and identify the danger attached to them.
5.  **040.02.02.06.05** -- List approach and landing illusions for slope of the
    runway, black-hole approach, and terrain around runway, and state the danger
    involved with recommendations to avoid or counteract the problems with high
    or low approach or flare at the wrong time.
6.  **040.02.02.06.06** -- State the problems associated with flickering lights
    (strobe lights, anti-collision lights, propellers and rotors under certain
    light conditions, etc.).
7.  **040.02.02.06.07** -- Describe vestibular illusions caused by the angular
    accelerations (the Leans, Coriolis) and linear accelerations (somatogravic,
    G-effect).
8.  **040.02.02.06.08** -- Relate the above-mentioned vestibular illusions to
    problems encountered in flight and state the dangers involved.
9.  **040.02.02.06.09** -- State that the "seat-of-the-pants" sense is
    completely unreliable when visual contact with the ground is lost or when
    flying in instrument meteorological conditions (IMC) or with a poor visual
    horizon.
10. **040.02.02.06.10** -- Differentiate between vertigo, Coriolis effect, and
    spatial disorientation.
11. **040.02.02.06.11** -- List the measures to prevent or overcome spatial
    disorientation.
