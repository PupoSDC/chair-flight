1.  **040.02.01.02.01** -- List the main components of the respiratory system
    and their function.
2.  **040.02.01.02.02** -- Identify the different volumes of air in the lungs
    and state the normal respiratory rate.
3.  **040.02.01.02.03** -- Explain the role of carbon dioxide in the control and
    regulation of respiration.
4.  **040.02.01.02.04** -- Describe the basic processes of external respiration
    and internal respiration.
5.  **040.02.01.02.05** -- List the factors that determine pulse rate.
6.  **040.02.01.02.06** -- Name the major components of the circulatory system
    and describe their function.
7.  **040.02.01.02.07** -- State the values for a normal pulse rate and the
    average cardiac output (heart rate Ã— stroke volume) of an adult at rest.
8.  **040.02.01.02.08** -- Define "systolic" and "diastolic" blood pressure.
9.  **040.02.01.02.09** -- State the normal blood pressure ranges and units of
    measurement.
10. **040.02.01.02.10** -- List the main constituents of blood and describe
    their functions.
11. **040.02.01.02.11** -- Stress the function of haemoglobin in the circulatory
    system.
12. **040.02.01.02.12** -- Define "anaemia" and state its common causes.
13. **040.02.01.02.13** -- Indicate the effect of increasing altitude on
    haemoglobin oxygen saturation.
14. **040.02.01.02.14** -- Define "hypertension" and "hypotension".
15. **040.02.01.02.15** -- List the effects that high and low blood pressure
    will have on some normal functions of the human body.
16. **040.02.01.02.16** -- State that both hypotension and hypertension may
    disqualify a pilot from obtaining medical clearance to fly.
17. **040.02.01.02.17** -- List the factors which can lead to hypertension for
    an individual.
18. **040.02.01.02.18** -- State the corrective actions that may be taken to
    reduce high blood pressure.
19. **040.02.01.02.19** -- Stress that hypertension is the major factor of
    strokes in the general population.
20. **040.02.01.02.20** -- Differentiate between "angina" and "heart attack".
21. **040.02.01.02.21** -- Explain the major risk factors for coronary disease.
22. **040.02.01.02.22** -- State the role physical exercise plays in reducing
    the chances of developing coronary disease.
23. **040.02.01.02.23** -- Define the two major forms of hypoxia (hypoxic and
    anaemic), and the common causes of both.
24. **040.02.01.02.24** -- State the symptoms of hypoxia.
25. **040.02.01.02.25** -- State that healthy people are able to compensate for
    altitudes up to approximately 10 000-12 000 ft.
26. **040.02.01.02.26** -- Name the three physiological thresholds and allocate
    the corresponding altitudes for each of them: reaction threshold (7 000 ft);
    disturbance threshold (10-12 000 ft); and critical threshold (22 000 ft).
27. **040.02.01.02.27** -- State the altitude at which short-term memory begins
    to be affected by hypoxia.
28. **040.02.01.02.28** -- Define the terms "time of useful consciousness" (TUC)
    and "effective performance time" (EPT).
29. **040.02.01.02.29** -- State that TUC varies among individuals, but the
    approximate values for a person seated (at rest) are: 20 000 ft 30 min; 30
    000 ft 1-2 min 35 000 ft; 30-90 s 40 000 ft 15-20 s
30. **040.02.01.02.30** -- List the factors that determine the severity of
    hypoxia.
31. **040.02.01.02.31** -- State the equivalent altitudes when breathing ambient
    air and 100 per cent oxygen at mean sea level (MSL) and at approximately 10
    000, 30 000 and 40 000 ft.
32. **040.02.01.02.32** -- Describe the role of carbon dioxide in
    hyperventilation.
33. **040.02.01.02.33** -- Define the term "hyperventilation".
34. **040.02.01.02.34** -- List the factors that cause hyperventilation.
35. **040.02.01.02.35** -- State that hyperventilation may be caused by
    psychological or physiological reasons.
36. **040.02.01.02.36** -- List the signs and symptoms of hyperventilation.
37. **040.02.01.02.37** -- List the measures which may be taken to counteract
    hyperventilation: breath slowly, close one opening of the nose, speak
    loudly, place a paper bag over nose and mouth.
38. **040.02.01.02.38** -- State the normal range of cabin pressure altitude in
    pressurised commercial air transport aircraft and describe its protective
    function for aircrew and passengers.
39. **040.02.01.02.39** -- List the vital actions the crew has to perform when
    cabin pressurisation is lost (oxygen mask on, emergency descent, land as
    soon as possible, and no further flight for the next minimum 24 hours).
    State that decompression sickness symptoms can occur up to 24 hours later.
40. **040.02.01.02.40** -- Identify the causes of decompression sickness in
    flight operation.
41. **040.02.01.02.41** -- State how decompression sickness can be prevented.
42. **040.02.01.02.42** -- List the symptoms of decompression sickness (bends,
    creeps, chokes, staggers).
43. **040.02.01.02.43** -- Indicate how decompression sickness may be treated.
44. **040.02.01.02.44** -- Define the hazards of diving and flying, and give the
    recommendations associated with these activities.
45. **040.02.01.02.45** -- Define "linear acceleration" and "angular
    acceleration".
46. **040.02.01.02.46** -- Describe the effects of z-acceleration on the
    circulation and blood volume distribution.
47. **040.02.01.02.47** -- List magnitude, duration and onset as factors that
    determine the effects of acceleration on the human body.
48. **040.02.01.02.48** -- List the effects of positive acceleration with
    respect to type, sequence and corresponding G-load.
49. **040.02.01.02.49** -- State how carbon monoxide is produced.
50. **040.02.01.02.50** -- State how the presence of carbon monoxide in the
    blood affects the distribution of oxygen.
51. **040.02.01.02.51** -- List the signs and symptoms of carbon-monoxide
    poisoning.
52. **040.02.01.02.52** -- Explain immediate countermeasures on suspicion of
    carbon-monoxide poisoning and how poisoning can be treated later on the
    ground.
